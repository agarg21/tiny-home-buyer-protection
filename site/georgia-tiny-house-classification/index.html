Classification first

Possible paths depend on the structure category

  • Possible path: classify the tiny home type first, then verify local zoning/siting permission with the relevant Georgia city or county.
  • Possible path: modular or site-built dwelling route where Georgia building code requirements and local zoning/siting rules are satisfied.
  • Possible path: RV, THOW, or park model route only where local planning/zoning ordinances allow the intended siting and use.
Classification is not permission.

Georgia DCA materials can help identify the type of structure and code questions to ask. They do not decide whether a specific local jurisdiction, parcel, HOA, lender, insurer, septic system, utility plan, or occupancy use will work.

Official sources

What the official sources say

DCA tiny house categories

Georgia DCA identifies four tiny home categories: RVs, manufactured/mobile homes, residential modular/industrialized buildings, and site-built dwellings.

Georgia DCA tiny house fact sheet

Local zoning still controls siting

DCA says local government zoning requirements vary widely and that prospective owners should obtain local permission to site the tiny home.

Georgia DCA tiny house fact sheet

Construction-code documents

DCA states that tiny house construction appendix provisions must be locally adopted to be enforced. DCA's construction-code documents page includes tiny house appendix materials and related state code documents.

Georgia DCA construction code documents

Before you buy

Verification checklist

Use this as a question list before buying land, ordering a tiny home, or relying on seller claims in Georgia.

  1. Classify the structure: RV, manufactured/mobile home, residential modular/industrialized building, site-built dwelling, THOW, park model, or another category.
  2. Ask the city or county planning/zoning office whether that category and intended use are allowed on the parcel.
  3. Ask the local building department which code path, foundation, inspection, and permit requirements apply.
  4. Ask about septic, water, utility, driveway, fire access, and health department requirements.
  5. Check HOA documents, deed restrictions, lender conditions, and insurance requirements.
  6. Save official URLs, staff names, dates, and written answers in a source log.
