Placement guide

Tiny house placement usually depends on the category

A seller may call many structures a "tiny home." Local offices often care about a more specific category.

THOW or RV-like structure

Possible path: RV park, tiny home community, temporary use permit, or limited accessory use if local rules allow it. Verify whether full-time residential use is treated differently from camping or temporary occupancy.

Park model RV

Possible path: a park, campground, or community designed for park models. Ask whether the local office treats it as an RV, dwelling, accessory structure, or something else for the intended use.

Foundation tiny home or ADU

Possible path: primary dwelling or accessory dwelling unit if zoning, building code, lot, utility, and occupancy rules fit. Local ADU ordinances and minimum dwelling standards can matter.

Modular or manufactured home

Possible path: manufactured or modular housing rules if the home meets the relevant certification, siting, foundation, zoning, and installation requirements. Confirm the exact classification before purchase.

Why city vs county matters

Land outside city limits may still be governed by county zoning, health department rules, state building codes, subdivision covenants, utility districts, fire access rules, and deed restrictions. If a property is inside a city, city zoning may be the first gate.

Why seller claims are not enough

Be careful with claims like "legal anywhere" or "no restrictions." Ask for official ordinance links, the parcel's zoning district, occupancy rules, utility requirements, and written confirmation from the relevant office before relying on the claim.

Tiny Home Placement Verification Checklist

Email capture is not connected yet because the provider is UNKNOWN. For now, use this on-page version before contacting local offices.

  1. Identify whether the parcel is inside city limits, unincorporated county land, a subdivision, a park, or a community.
  2. Find the zoning district and allowed residential, accessory, RV, manufactured, modular, temporary, and rental uses.
  3. Ask planning or building staff about your specific structure type, intended use, and occupancy length.
  4. Ask the health department about septic, water, toilet, and wastewater requirements before buying land.
  5. Check HOA documents, deed restrictions, lender conditions, and insurer requirements before signing.
  6. Save official URLs, staff names, dates, and written answers in a source log.
